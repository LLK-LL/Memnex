# Story And Figures

## Purpose

Use this reference for abstracts, motivation, contribution statements, figure/table narration, and defense-slide storytelling.

## Abstract Shape

For a midterm report or thesis abstract, use 5 to 7 sentences:

1. Field context
2. Specific problem
3. Existing gap
4. Proposed research route
5. Completed work or preliminary result
6. Expected contribution or next stage
7. Application or academic value, if concrete

Avoid opening with broad slogans. The first two sentences should let a reader know the exact research area.

## Motivation

A strong motivation paragraph answers:

- What bottleneck exists?
- Who or what is affected by it?
- Why do existing methods not fully solve it?
- Why is the proposed route plausible?

Do not rely on "杩戝勾鏉?..骞挎硾鍏虫敞" unless followed by the concrete reason.

## Contribution Statement

Use contribution bullets only when useful. Each bullet should contain:

- object: what was built/designed/proposed
- mechanism: how it works at a high level
- evidence: how it is or will be evaluated

## Figure Narration

When describing a technical route figure:

1. State the figure's purpose.
2. Explain the main modules from left to right or top to bottom.
3. Explain the key data/control flow.
4. Connect the figure back to the research goal.

Avoid merely naming boxes in the figure.

## Table Narration

When describing a result table:

1. Name the comparison target.
2. Identify the primary metric.
3. State the pattern, not every number.
4. Explain the technical reason or limitation.

If the table is preliminary, say so.

## Defense Slide Story

For a 10 to 15 minute midterm defense:

| Slide | Main point |
|---|---|
| 1 | Title, student, supervisor |
| 2 | Research background |
| 3 | Problem and gap |
| 4 | Research objectives |
| 5 | Technical route |
| 6 | Key method/module |
| 7 | Completed work |
| 8 | Preliminary results |
| 9 | Problems and risks |
| 10 | Next plan |

Each slide needs one spoken takeaway. Do not place full report paragraphs on slides.
